Dear Editors,

We submit for your consideration the manuscript "A substrate recursion
principle for biological information, with empirical anchoring through a
templating-mode taxonomy" for publication in PRX Life as an Article.

The paper specifies, at the substrate level, what physical conditions a
substrate-operation pair must satisfy to support open-ended
information-generating recursion. Four structural conditions are stated and
proved jointly necessary: positive entropy per recognized site, unboundedly
many independently addressable non-recurrent sites, same-operation recursion
(possibly via reversible involution), and heritable local drift in the
recognized alphabet. A fifth scope-defining condition demarcates the
non-teleological domain in which the structural conditions apply. From the
principle, two corollaries follow: a substrate-mode taxonomy classifying the
six known biological templating mechanisms (DNA replication, ribosomal
translation, prion propagation, modular biosynthesis, ciliate cortical
inheritance, and the cyclic-conformational class instantiated by Drt3b),
and a population-dynamic plateau result establishing that mechanisms failing
same-operation recursion or heritable local drift at the individual level
are structurally capped at mechanism-specific ceilings.

The work is suited to PRX Life on the journal's stated criteria. It connects
information theory, statistical physics of finite populations, and structural
biochemistry to address a question of substrate-level physics that has been
posed informally since Schrödinger but never given a formal structural
specification. The framework is empirically anchored on the recently
characterized Drt3b system (Deng et al., Science 2026), where the apparatus
quantitatively matches the published E26Q observation (analytical prediction
marginal G fraction = 0.10; observed = 0.1016) and family-level apparatus
application across 1,232 Drt3b homologs confirms the predicted selectivity-
gate signature for the six naturally occurring E26→D clades. Two universal-
gate residues (R253 and G248) generate single-experiment falsification
targets through site-directed mutagenesis with structurally distinct
predicted signatures.

The empirical work in this paper is computational; the wet-lab anchor is
the published Deng et al. dataset. The R253A and G248A predictions are
proposed for experimental verification by the structural biology community
and would constitute the strongest direct test of the framework's
architectural-vs-selectivity gate distinction.

The manuscript reports pre-registered simulations (M0–M4 inheritance
mechanisms, apparatus calibration sweeps, family-level homolog analysis).
All simulation code, raw outputs, and analysis pipelines will be deposited
on Zenodo at acceptance.

The paper has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration
at any other journal. We declare no competing interests.
