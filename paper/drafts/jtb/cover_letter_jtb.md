Dear Editors,

We submit our manuscript, "A substrate recursion principle for biological information, with empirical anchoring through a templating-mode taxonomy," for consideration as a research article in the *Journal of Theoretical Biology*.

Biology transmits information across generations through a small number of chemically distinct templating reactions — DNA replication, translation, prion propagation, modular biosynthesis, and the inherited ciliate cortex among them. The recently characterized bacterial defence system Drt3b adds another: it synthesizes alternating poly(AC) DNA without using a nucleic acid template, through a cyclic active-site mechanism that does not fit the textbook picture of inheritance. The field has lacked a way to say, at the level of the catalytic reaction itself, which such chemistries can support open-ended inheritance and which cannot. We provide that account and use it to classify the known biology.

We give the physicochemical conditions a template–catalyst pair must satisfy, and separate two requirements the prior literature conflated: the capacity to transmit many heritable configurations, and the generation of new ones. A corollary sorts six attested biological templating systems into a taxonomy keyed to which condition each fails. The framework anchors quantitatively to the Drt3 reverse-transcriptase system: it classifies Drt3b as a cyclic two-state channel with a one-bit heredity ceiling, predicts 10% dG misincorporation for the Glu26-to-Gln active-site mutant against a published 10.16%, and recovers a 15.7-fold dG elevation across six natural Glu26-to-Asp clades among 1,232 homologs. Two single-residue active-site substitutions (Arg253-to-Ala, Gly248-to-Ala) provide single-experiment site-directed-mutagenesis tests that would refute the framework's central claim if their measured effects disagreed.

The work is theoretical in method but biological in aim and consequence: its contribution is a structural account of biological inheritance and a classification of real templating systems with experimentally testable predictions, not a mathematical result for its own sake. We have kept the formal statements and proofs in a supplementary mathematical appendix so the main text remains intelligible to biologists across fields, in line with the journal's guidance.
